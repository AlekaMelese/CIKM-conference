
================================================================================
ANNOTATION GUIDELINES: Clinical Validation of Factual Grounding
                       in LLM-Generated Discharge Summaries
================================================================================

TASK OVERVIEW
=============
You will evaluate LLM-generated clinical discharge summaries as a whole.
For each summary, you will be shown side-by-side:

  1. The SOURCE CLINICAL NOTE (ground truth written by the physician)
  2. The GENERATED SUMMARY (structured discharge summary to evaluate)

Your task is to determine whether the generated summary is factually
grounded in the source clinical note.

IMPORTANT: Evaluate the summary holistically — consider whether the clinical
facts across all sections are supported by the source note. If a section
contains information you cannot verify from the source note, factor that
into your overall grounding judgment.


ABOUT THE DATASET
=================
The clinical texts you will evaluate come from a DE-IDENTIFIED dataset. The dataset contains NO personal
health information — patient names, physician names, exact dates, admission
numbers, addresses, and other identifiers were REMOVED before the data was
released.

Because of this de-identification process, you may notice:
- Readability, grammar, and sentence flow can look "wrong" in places
- Dates are stripped — prepositions like "in", "on", "at", "from" may
  be followed by nothing (e.g., "admitted in ,")
- Some proper nouns are replaced with placeholders ("___", "[**...**]")
  or simply removed, leaving small gaps in the sentence
- Sentences sometimes appear truncated where a scrubbed PHI element sat

These are PRE-PROCESSING ARTIFACTS, not clinical errors or hallucinations.
They appear in BOTH Text A and Text B, so they cancel out when you compare
the two versions. Your job is to assess the CLINICAL CONTENT — diagnoses,
medications, dosages, findings — not the text quality.


EVALUATION CRITERIA
===================

1. FACTUAL GROUNDING (Required)
   Choose one:
   - "Fully Supported"  — All clinical facts in the summary can be verified
     from the source note. Medications, diagnoses, procedures, lab values,
     and clinical findings all match across all sections.
   - "Partially Supported" — Some facts are verifiable from the source note,
     but parts are unverifiable, imprecise, or missing context. For example,
     a correct diagnosis with a wrong date, or correct medication with
     missing dosage.
   - "Unsupported" — The summary contains clinical information that
     contradicts or cannot be found in the source note. This includes
     fabricated medications, wrong diagnoses, invented lab values, or
     procedures not mentioned in the source.
   - "Cannot Determine" — Insufficient information in the source note to judge.
     Use this sparingly.

   TIP — AUTO-FILL BEHAVIOR:
   To save time, the UI auto-fills related fields based on your choice here
   AND on Q3 (Error Type):
     Factual Grounding = "Fully Supported"
        → Error Type auto-set to "None"
        → Clinical Severity auto-set to "None"
     Factual Grounding = "Unsupported"
        → "None" is removed from Error Type choices (error required:
          Fabrication / Inaccuracy / Omission)
     Error Type = "None" (set manually)
        → Factual Grounding auto-set to "Fully Supported"
        → Clinical Severity auto-set to "None"
   The auto-fill is only a suggestion. You can still change any field
   when your clinical judgment disagrees. Always verify all required
   fields match your actual assessment before submitting.

2. GROUNDING CONFIDENCE (Required)
   How confident are you in your Factual Grounding judgment above?
   Rate 1-5:
   1 = Very uncertain — the source note is unclear or incomplete,
       making it hard to verify the generated content
   2 = Somewhat uncertain — you can partially verify but some
       parts remain ambiguous
   3 = Moderately confident — most facts are verifiable but
       a few are unclear
   4 = Confident — you are fairly sure of your grounding judgment
       with only minor uncertainty
   5 = Very confident — the evidence clearly supports your judgment
       (e.g., obvious match or obvious contradiction)

3. ERROR TYPE (Required if Unsupported; optional if Partially Supported)
   Choose one:
   - "None" — No error found.
   - "Fabrication" — Information that does not exist in the source note at all.
     This includes invented facts, made-up medications, fabricated lab values,
     or general medical knowledge added by the model that was not in this
     patient's note.
     Examples: medication never prescribed, invented vital signs, stating
     "lisinopril is an ACE inhibitor" when the note only says "lisinopril"
   - "Inaccuracy" — Information that exists in the source note but is wrong,
     altered, or misrepresented in the generated summary. This includes wrong
     dosages, wrong dates, swapped laterality, admission findings reported as
     discharge status, or information that may have come from another patient.
     Examples: metoprolol 50mg instead of 25mg, admission temperature reported
     as discharge temperature, surgical history not found in this patient's note
   - "Omission" — Critical qualifying information from the source note is
     dropped, changing the clinical meaning of the summary.
     Examples: "hypertension" instead of "well-controlled hypertension on
     lisinopril", dropping "rapid ventricular response" from atrial
     fibrillation diagnosis

4. CLINICAL SEVERITY (Required)
   Choose one:
   - "None" — No meaningful error detected
   - "Minor" — Clinically insignificant
     Examples: Minor wording differences, formatting issues, redundant
     information, trivially wrong detail that wouldn't affect care
   - "Major" — Clinically significant but unlikely to cause immediate harm
     Examples: Wrong date of procedure, incorrect specialist name, lab value
     from wrong date, attribution of finding to wrong imaging study
   - "Critical" — Could directly cause patient harm if acted upon
     Examples: Wrong medication/dosage, incorrect allergy, wrong procedure
     side, dangerous drug interaction missed, incorrect diagnosis leading
     to wrong treatment

5. CLARITY (Required, 1-5 Likert)
   How clear, readable, and well-organized is the generated summary?
   1 = Very poor — disorganized, incoherent, or difficult to follow
   2 = Poor — multiple unclear passages or confusing structure
   3 = Acceptable — understandable but could be improved
   4 = Good — clear and well-organized with minor issues
   5 = Excellent — clear, well-structured, and easy to read

6. CLINICAL UTILITY (Required, 1-5 Likert)
   Would a clinician use this summary for patient handoff or discharge?
   1 = Not usable — would need to be rewritten entirely
   2 = Barely usable — requires major edits before clinical use
   3 = Usable with edits — needs moderate corrections
   4 = Mostly usable — minor edits needed, clinically adequate
   5 = Ready to use — suitable for clinical handoff as-is

7. DOCUMENT-LEVEL COMPLETENESS (Optional)
   In addition to factual grounding, evaluate the summary for completeness.
   - "Crucial Omission" — Did the generated summary entirely miss a critical diagnosis,
     medication, or procedure that was prominent in the source note?
   - "Sufficient" — The summary captured all necessary, clinically relevant elements
     from the source note without major omissions.

8. FREE-TEXT NOTES (Optional)
   Any additional observations, e.g.:
   - What specific error you identified
   - Why you found it difficult to judge
   - Domain-specific context that informed your decision

SECTION-SPECIFIC GUIDANCE
=========================

Different clinical sections carry different risk levels. Keep these in mind:

HIGH-RISK SECTIONS (errors here are often Critical or Major):
  - Medications: Wrong drug, dosage, frequency, or route
  - Assessment/Impression: Wrong diagnosis or clinical reasoning
  - Chief Complaint: Wrong reason for admission

MEDIUM-RISK SECTIONS (errors often Major or Minor):
  - HPI: Distorted timeline or symptoms
  - Labs/Imaging: Wrong values or wrong test attribution
  - Physical Examination: Wrong findings

LOWER-RISK SECTIONS (errors often Minor):
  - Discharge Condition: Wording differences
  - Follow-Up: Scheduling details
  - Case Type: Classification differences


ABOUT THE SOURCE NOTES
======================
The source clinical notes are written by physicians and may not always
have clear or consistent section headers. Some clinical information
(e.g., follow-up plans, medication changes, diagnoses) may appear
without a labeled section — embedded within "Brief hospital course",
"Discharge instructions", or as free text. Use Ctrl+F / Cmd+F to
search for specific terms when verifying facts.


GENERAL INSTRUCTIONS
====================
- Evaluate each summary as a whole, considering all 11 sections together
- Focus on CLINICAL accuracy, not grammatical style or formatting
- Section headers and formatting artifacts should not count against grounding
- Consider medical abbreviations as equivalent to their expanded forms
  (e.g., "HTN" = "hypertension", "DM" = "diabetes mellitus")
- Compare the generated summary directly against the source clinical note
  to verify every clinical fact

ABSTRACTIVE SUMMARIZATION NOTE:
These summaries are generated using abstractive (not extractive)
summarization. Key characteristics to be aware of:

  1. PARAPHRASING: The model rephrases clinical information in its own
     words. The generated text will NOT match the source note
     word-for-word.

  2. CONDENSATION: The model compresses long clinical notes into shorter
     structured summaries. Not every detail from the source note will
     appear in the summary.

  3. TOKEN LIMITS: The models have a maximum output length. Very long
     source notes may result in summaries where later sections are
     shorter or less detailed.

Use your clinical judgment to evaluate the generated summary.

IMPORTANT: You are evaluating whether the GENERATED SUMMARY is factually
grounded in the SOURCE NOTE. Consider all sections holistically when
assigning the overall grounding label.


EXAMPLE ANNOTATIONS
===================

Example 1 (Fully Supported):
  In the Chief Complaint section, the generated summary states:
  "Patient presented with chest pain radiating to the left arm"
  The source note contains: "CC: chest pain, radiating to left arm"
  -> Grounding: Fully Supported
  -> Confidence: 5
  -> Error type: None
  -> Severity: None
  -> Clarity: 5
  -> Clinical Utility: 5

Example 2 (Fabrication — info not in source):
  In the Assessment section, the generated summary states:
  "Patient was diagnosed with acute pancreatitis"
  But the source note says: "assessment: acute cholecystitis"
  -> Grounding: Unsupported (pancreatitis vs cholecystitis)
  -> Confidence: 5
  -> Error type: Fabrication
  -> Severity: Critical (wrong diagnosis)
  -> Clarity: 4
  -> Clinical Utility: 1 (wrong diagnosis makes it dangerous)

Example 3 (Fabrication — added medical knowledge):
  In the Medications section, the generated summary states:
  "Lisinopril, an ACE inhibitor for blood pressure control, was continued"
  The source note only says: "discharge meds: lisinopril 10mg daily"
  -> Grounding: Partially Supported
  -> Confidence: 4
  -> Error type: Fabrication (the ACE inhibitor detail is not in this patient's note)
  -> Severity: Minor (true info but not from the source)

Example 4 (Inaccuracy — wrong dosage):
  In the Medications section, the generated summary states:
  "Patient was discharged on metoprolol 50mg twice daily"
  But the source note says: "discharge medications: metoprolol 25mg BID"
  -> Grounding: Partially Supported (dosage wrong: 50mg vs 25mg)
  -> Confidence: 5
  -> Error type: Inaccuracy
  -> Severity: Critical (wrong medication dosage)

Example 5 (Inaccuracy — wrong temporal context):
  In the Discharge Condition section, the generated summary states:
  "Patient was febrile with temperature 101.2F"
  But the source note says: "admission vitals: T 101.2F...
  discharge vitals: T 98.6F afebrile"
  -> Grounding: Unsupported (admission finding reported as discharge status)
  -> Confidence: 5
  -> Error type: Inaccuracy
  -> Severity: Major (wrong context could affect clinical decisions)

Example 6 (Inaccuracy — info possibly from another patient):
  In the Past Medical History section, the generated summary states:
  "History of Type 2 diabetes and bilateral knee replacement"
  But the source note says: "PMH: Type 2 diabetes, GERD, hypertension"
  -> Grounding: Partially Supported (diabetes correct, knee replacement not in source)
  -> Confidence: 4
  -> Error type: Inaccuracy
  -> Severity: Major (wrong surgical history)

Example 7 (Omission):
  In the Assessment section, the generated summary states:
  "Patient has atrial fibrillation"
  But the source note says: "new-onset atrial fibrillation with rapid
  ventricular response, rate 140s, started on IV amiodarone drip"
  -> Grounding: Partially Supported (diagnosis correct but critical details dropped)
  -> Confidence: 4
  -> Error type: Omission
  -> Severity: Major (dropped severity and treatment, changing clinical picture)

Example 8 (Document-Level Completeness):
  The source note mentions: sepsis, acute kidney injury, intubation, pressors
  The generated summary covers: sepsis, intubation, pressors
  -> Document Completeness: Crucial Omission (acute kidney injury entirely missing)
